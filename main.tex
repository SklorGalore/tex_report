\section{Converter Operation}

This project utilizes a full-bridge series resonant LLC converter with synchronous rectification.
The converter topology was chosen due to its ability to achieve soft switching under both switch turn on and switch turn off.
The converter can be seen below in figure \ref{fig:llcsr}.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/LLCSR}
	\caption{The full-bridge LLC series resonant converter with synchronous rectifier.}
	\label{fig:llcsr}
\end{figure}

This converter uses eight switches operating in four pairs of two complementary switches, two pairs in the high side inverter and two pairs in the low side rectifier.
In addition, these switches operate synchronously across the inverter and rectifier meaning that although there are eight switches, there are only two distinct physical states.


\subsection{Switching Modes}

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/mode1}
	\caption{The first switching state with switches 1, 4, 5, and 8 on.}
	\label{fig:mode1}
\end{figure}

During the first switching mode, the first two pairs of switches, 1, 4, 5, and 8, are on generating the positive portion of the square wave from the source and charging the resonant tank which is resonating at the primary resonant frequency $f_{r1}$.
This physical state exists until a time $t_1$ at which the current switches are opened, and the other switches are closed, following a dead time $t_1\rightarrow t_2$.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/mode2b}
	\caption{The second switching state with switches 2, 3, 6, and 7 on.}
	\label{fig:mode2b}
\end{figure}

During this stage, a similar but mirrored process occurs where the second two pairs of switches, 2, 3, 6, 7, are generating the negative portion of the square wave from the source and sending energy through the resonant tank and to the low side rectifier.
This state will exist until a time $t_3$ at which this combination of switches will be opened and a dead time will exist until the cycle resets at the end of the switching period.
In order to achieve soft switching through zero current and zero voltage switching methods, the LLC resonant tank and transformer are utilized by choosing a switching frequency to achieve a desired outcome.

\section{Resonant Modes}
The  function of the resonant tank and transformer are detailed below and take place across four distinct switching modes.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/converter_waveforms}
	\caption{Converter wave forms across one switching period}
	\label{fig:converterwaveforms}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Resonant Mode 1}
Resonant mode 1 occurs between $0<t<t_1$ where the first set of switches begins closed.
During this time, the magnetizing inductor current rises will the resonant inductor current rises and falls sinusoidally.
Eventually at $t=t_1$, the resonant inductor current will meet the magnetizing inductor current.
When this occurs, the current flow from the high side to the low side of the transformer will be zero.
In this moment, there will be no current flowing through the low side active switches and they can be opened under zero current.

\subsection{Resonant Mode 2}

In resonant mode 2, all switches are open, and the resonant tank begins to resonate at the frequency associated with the magnetizing inductor.
Following a dead time, the output capacitance of the secondary pairs of MOSFETs will discharge to zero and the body diodes will clamp across them, allowing the next set of switches to be closed under zero voltage at time $t=t_2$.
\subsection{Resonant Mode 3}

Similarly to the first resonant mode, current will vary through the resonant inductor and the magnetizing inductor current will fall until the two are once again equal.
At this point, $t=t_3$, the current through the transformer will return to zero and the active switches can be opened under zero current.
\subsection{Resonant Mode 4}

Similarly to mode 2, the final open switch capacitances will discharge, clamping their body diodes across them, and allowing them to be closed under zero voltage.
\section{Equivalenced Circuits}

For ease of derivation and to simplify the necessary equations, the full bridge LLC series resonant converter can be simplified as a voltage source, LLC resonant tank, and constant impedance load.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/LLC_equiv-2}
	\caption{The equivalenced converter containing a square wave generator, resonant tank, and load resistance.}
	\label{fig:llcequiv}
\end{figure}

The inverter's output can be considered a square wave, while the resistive load can be passed through the rectifier and transformer to create an equivalent high side resistor connected to the tank.
While this circuit is much more approachable than the full converter, the harmonics composing the square wave generator can be further simplified.
By neglecting the effect of harmonics beyond the first, an equivalent idealized AC source can be constructed. 
\begin{gather*}
	V_{in}=\sum_{m=1,3,5...}^\infty\frac{4V_{s}}{\pi m}\sin\omega t m\\
	V_{s,pk}=\frac{4V_s}{\pi}\rightarrow V_{s,RMS}=\frac{4V_s}{\sqrt{2}\pi}
\end{gather*}

By taking the same assumptions to the load, an equivalently behaved $R_AC$ can be used.
\begin{gather*}
	V_{out}=\sum_{m=1,3,5...}^\infty\frac{4V_{out}}{\pi m}\sin\omega t m\\
	V_{out,pk}=\frac{4V_{out}}{\pi}\rightarrow V_{out,RMS}=\frac{4V_{out}}{\sqrt{2}\pi}\\
	I_{out}=\text{avg}|I_{s,pk}\sin\omega t|\\
	I_{out}=\frac{2}{\pi}I_{s,pk}\rightarrow I_{s,pk}=I_{out}\frac{\pi}{2}\\
	I_{out,RMS}=I_{out}\frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}}\\
	R_{AC}= \frac{V_{out,RMS}}{I_{out,RMS}}=\frac{4V_{out}}{\sqrt{2}\pi}\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{I_{out}\pi}\\
	R_{AC}=\frac{8}{\pi^2}R\\
	R_{pri,AC}=\frac{8n^2}{\pi^2}R
\end{gather*}
This circuit is much easier to analyze and can facilitate deriving the converter gain.
\section{Gain Derivation}

When deriving the gain of the converter, we treat the circuit as a voltage divider and seek the voltage drop across the magnetizing inductance and equivalent AC resistance as our output in terms of the tank parameters and input voltage.
Some common quantities are defined first
\[Q = \frac{\sqrt{L_rC_r^{-1}}}{R_{AC}}\quad f^\prime = \frac{f_{sw}}{f_r}\quad K = \frac{L_m}{L_r}\]

Next, using the base formula for a voltage divider, the gain is calculated.
\begin{gather*}
	V_{out} = V_{in}\frac{Z_{out}}{Z_{out}+Z_{in}}
\end{gather*}
