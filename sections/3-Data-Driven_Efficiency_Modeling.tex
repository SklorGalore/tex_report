The dataset was generated through large-scale time-domain simulations using a detailed LLC converter model implemented in MATLAB/Simulink. The main operating parameters were systematically varied within predefined ranges, including input voltage ($V_{in} \in \{400, 500, 600, 700, 800, 900\}\,\text{V}$), output voltage ($V_o \in \{400, 600, 800\}\,\text{V}$), output power ($P_{out} \in \{8, 10, 12\}\,\text{kW}$), resonant frequency ($f_r \in \{120, 130, 140\}\,\text{kHz}$), and quality factor ($Q \in \{0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6\}$). \hl{Cite any relevant works that have used similar MATLAB/Simulink-based approaches to create datasets.}

For each operating condition, the switching frequency ($f_s$) was swept within a $\pm 1\%$ range around the resonant frequency using seven equally spaced points (three below, one at resonance, and three above), ensuring that the analysis captures the region close to resonance, which is critical for high-efficiency operation. A total of 4536 scenarios were simulated, generating approximately 10~GB of data and resulting in a comprehensive dataset that captures the complex relationships between design parameters and converter efficiency. \hl{Cite any relevant works that have used similar MATLAB/Simulink-based approaches to create datasets.}

Each scenario was simulated for a duration of 10~ms, allowing the system to reach steady-state conditions. The relevant performance metrics were extracted from the last 1~ms of the simulation to ensure that transient effects were excluded from the analysis. The input and output power were computed by averaging the instantaneous power over the steady-state interval, and the efficiency was calculated as the ratio between the average output power and the average input power.

Since the study focuses on parameter design, all time-domain signals were reduced to representative scalar features using either average or root mean square (RMS) values, depending on the nature of each signal. For instance, the inductor current ($i_{Lr}$), the resonant tank voltage ($V_{AB}$), the secondary voltage ($V_{Sec}$), the primary voltage ($V_{Pri}$), and the input current ($i_{In}$) were converted to RMS values, while the output voltage ($V_o$) and output power ($P_{out}$) were represented by their average values.

This feature extraction process resulted in a structured dataset containing 4536 samples (approximately 1.249~MB), where each sample includes the relevant features for training the machine learning model. At the end, a pre-processing step was performed to clean the dataset by removing the outliers and any non-physical results, such as efficiencies outside the range $0 \leq \eta \leq 1$. The cleaned dataset was then organized into a structured format suitable for machine learning, with each row representing a unique operating condition and its corresponding efficiency.

Subsequently, 3 different machine learning models were evaluated, including Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), Random Forest (RF), and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN). The machine learning models used in this work estimate the converter efficiency as a function of the operating conditions. The input feature vector is defined as:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{x} = [V_{in}, V_o, P_{out}, f_s, f_r, Z_r, k, Q]
\end{align}

where $Z_r$ denotes the characteristic impedance of the resonant tank, defined as $Z_r = \sqrt{L_r/C_r}$, and $k$ represents the inductance ratio given by $k = L_m / L_r$.

\subsection{Multilayer Perceptron (MLP)}

\hl{Explain why these hyperparameters were chosen. Cite any relevant works that have used similar hyperparameters for MLP training.}
The Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) was evaluated as a nonlinear regression model to approximate the converter efficiency. The network consists of two hidden layers with 128 and 64 neurons, respectively, and employs the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function to capture the nonlinear relationships between the operating conditions and the efficiency.

The MLP model approximates the efficiency as a composition of linear transformations and nonlinear activation functions, given by:
\begin{align}
\eta &= f_{\mathrm{MLP}}(\mathbf{x}) \nonumber \\
     &= W_3\,\sigma\!\left(W_2\,\sigma\!\left(W_1 \mathbf{x} + b_1\right) + b_2\right) + b_3
\end{align}

where $\mathbf{x}$ represents the input feature vector, $W_i$ and $b_i$ are the weight matrices and bias vectors of each layer, respectively, and $\sigma(\cdot)$ denotes the activation function.

The model was trained using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.1. Early stopping was employed to prevent overfitting, with training terminated if no improvement was observed over 30 consecutive iterations, up to a maximum of 1500 epochs.

\subsection{Random Forest (RF)}

\hl{Explain why these hyperparameters were chosen. Cite any relevant works that have used similar hyperparameters for RF training.}
A Random Forest (RF) regressor was employed to approximate the converter efficiency as a function of the operating conditions. The model consists of an ensemble of decision trees, where each tree is trained using a bootstrap sample of the dataset and a random subset of features.

The RF model predicts the efficiency as the average output of all decision trees in the ensemble:
\begin{align}
    \eta &= f_{\mathrm{RF}}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} T_i(\mathbf{x})
\end{align}

where $\mathbf{x}$ is the input feature vector, $T_i(\mathbf{x})$ represents the prediction of the $i$-th decision tree, and $N$ is the total number of trees in the forest.

In this work, the model was configured with $N = 300$ trees, a maximum depth of 15, and a minimum of 5 samples required to split an internal node. These hyperparameters were selected to provide a balance between model complexity and generalization capability.

\subsection{K Nearest Neighbors (KNN)}

\hl{Explain why these hyperparameters were chosen. Cite any relevant works that have used similar hyperparameters for KNN training.}
The K Nearest Neighbors (KNN) algorithm was also evaluated as a non-parametric regression model to estimate the converter efficiency. In this approach, the prediction for a given input is obtained based on the $k$ closest samples in the feature space.

The KNN model estimates the efficiency as the average value of the target variable among the $k$ nearest neighbors:
\begin{align}
    \eta &= f_{\mathrm{KNN}}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x})} y_i
\end{align}

where $\mathbf{x}$ is the input feature vector, $\mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x})$ represents the set of the $k$ nearest neighbors of $\mathbf{x}$ in the training dataset, and $y_i$ is the efficiency associated with each neighbor.

In this work, the model was configured with $k = 5$ neighbors. This choice provides a balance between noise reduction and local approximation capability.

\subsection{Model Performance Evaluation}

To evaluate the performance of the machine learning models, standard regression metrics were used, including the coefficient of determination ($R^2$) and the prediction error.

The models were trained and tested using the processed dataset, and their performance was compared in terms of accuracy and generalization capability. Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} summarizes the results obtained for each model.

Among the evaluated approaches, the Random Forest model achieved the highest accuracy, with $R^2 = 0.9959$ and an average prediction error below 1\%. In contrast, the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) models presented lower performance, with $R^2$ values of 0.7988 and 0.8227, respectively, and significantly higher prediction errors.

Additionally, point-wise validation was performed using randomly selected samples from the dataset. For instance, for a given operating condition, the real efficiency was 0.410625, while the Random Forest model predicted 0.410492, resulting in a relative error of only 0.032\%.

These results indicate that the Random Forest model provides a highly accurate approximation of the nonlinear relationship between design parameters and converter efficiency, making it suitable for fast design space exploration.

Based on these results, the Random Forest model was selected to approximate the efficiency of the converter as a nonlinear function of the operating conditions:

\begin{align}
\eta &= f_{\mathrm{RF}}(V_{in}, V_o, P_{out}, f_s, f_r, Z_r, k, Q)
\end{align}

This model enables rapid evaluation of the design space without requiring time-consuming simulations.

To identify optimal operating points, the design problem is formulated as the maximization of the converter efficiency subject to physical operating constraints. Instead of explicitly enforcing these constraints, a penalty-based objective function is defined as:

\begin{align}
    J &= \eta - \alpha \frac{|f_r - f_s|}{f_s} - \beta \frac{|Z_r - R_{ac}|}{R_{ac}}
\end{align}
where $\alpha = 0.3$ and $\beta = 0.5$ are empirically selected weighting factors that balance efficiency maximization with physical feasibility. The penalty terms enforce operation close to the resonant frequency and proper impedance matching, which are known to be critical conditions for high-efficiency operation in LLC converters.

The optimal operating point is obtained by solving:

\begin{align}
    \max_{f_s,\,Z_r} \; J
\end{align}

This formulation enables the identification of high-efficiency operating regions while implicitly satisfying physical constraints, significantly reducing computational effort compared to traditional simulation-based design approaches. \hl{Cite any relevant works that have used similar approaches to multi-objective optimization.}
