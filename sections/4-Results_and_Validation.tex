For the validation of the proposed methodology, a LLC which the parameters in Table~\ref{tab:LLC_Parameters} was designed using the data-driven efficiency mapping framework. The converter was simulated in a time-domain circuit simulator to evaluate its performance under steady-state conditions, with a focus on efficiency and waveform characteristics.

The data-driven design process identified the optimal resonant tank parameters according to the specified design requirements. The values of the resonant inductor ($L_r$) and capacitor ($C_r$) were determined to achieve operation close to the resonant frequency while maximizing efficiency. The resulting design was shown in Table~\ref{tab:AI_Parameters}, which includes the predicted efficiency.

The designed converter was simulated under the specification operation conditions, and the resulting waveforms were analyzed to validate the predicted performance. The key waveforms of interest include the input and output voltages and currents, the swtiches voltages and currents, used to calculated the instantaneus power lossses, and the efficiency.

Fig.~\ref{fig:Result_1} presents the simulated waveforms of the input voltage ($V_{in}$), input current ($i_{in}$), output voltage ($V_o$), and output current ($I_o$) for the designed LLC resonant converter operating at approximately 10~kW. The waveforms are captured under steady-state conditions, with the converter operating close to the resonant frequency. The input current exhibits a pulsating behavior characteristic of resonant converters, reflecting the energy transfer through the tank. The output voltage and current remain nearly constant with low ripple, indicating stable operation and effective filtering at the output stage.

Fig.~\ref{fig:Result_2} presents the steady-state waveforms of the input voltage ($V_{in}$), input current ($i_{in}$), inverter output voltage ($V_{AB}$), and resonant inductor current ($i_{Lr}$) for the LLC converter operating at approximately 10~kW. The input voltage remains constant, while the input current exhibits a pulsating behavior associated with the power transfer through the resonant tank. The inverter output voltage $V_{AB}$ shows a square waveform due to the full-bridge switching operation. In contrast, the resonant inductor current $i_{Lr}$ is nearly sinusoidal, indicating that the converter operates close to its resonant frequency. This operating condition promotes efficient energy transfer and is consistent with soft-switching operation, contributing to reduced switching losses.

Fig.~\ref{fig:Result_3} shows the voltage and current waveforms of the switches ($Q_1$-$Q_4$) during steady-state operation. The switch voltages exhibit the expected square-wave behavior associated with the full-bridge topology, while the corresponding currents follow a quasi-sinusoidal profile imposed by the resonant tank. It can be observed that the current through each switch naturally goes to zero before commutation, indicating soft-switching conditions. This behavior reduces switching losses and electromagnetic stress on the devices, contributing to improved converter efficiency. The symmetry between the switches also confirms balanced operation of the full-bridge converter.

Fig.~\ref{fig:Result_5} presents the switching frequency, input and output power, and the resulting efficiency under steady-state operation. The switching frequency remains constant, indicating stable control conditions. The input and output power profiles are also nearly constant, with a small difference corresponding to the system losses.

The efficiency remains stable around 98.9\%, confirming that the converter operates under high-efficiency conditions with limited power dissipation. The consistency of these quantities over time validates both the steady-state operation and the reliability of the computed efficiency.
