\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}

Recent growth and adoption of data centers, electric vehicles (EVs), and renewable energy systems (REs) has increased the demand for highly efficient, power-dense, power electronic converters. Among the various converter topologies, soft-switching resonant converters have emerged as a common solution due to their ability to achieve both zero voltage (ZVS) and zero current (ZCS) switching, which significantly reduces switching losses and improves overall efficiency \cite{11203780, chakraborty2019dc}.

Traditional design methodologies for soft-switching resonant converters remain complex and rely on approximating converter harmonics using first harmonic approximations (FHA) or complex time-domain analytical models \hl{Cite?}. These designs may provide quick starting points, but can suffer from inaccuracy under light loading conditions, non-linear loads, or when operation of the converter is not near the resonant frequency. While time-domain analytical models may address some of these inaccuracies, they are time-consuming and computationally expensive which makes them impractical for large-scale optimization of multiple parameters or iterative design approaches.

\hl{ How much time traditional ways take? Cite?}

In order to address these challenges, this paper proposes a data-driven efficiency mapping framework for rapid resonant converter design.
By utilizing comprehensive datasets generated through automated simulations, a predictive model capable of mapping circuit design parameters such as resonant tank inductance and capacitance, switching frequency, and desired input and output voltages, directly to an expected converter efficiency was developed. Through this approach, the need for iterative high computational cost computations can be bypassed allowing for accelerated design time.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A data-driven framework for rapid, efficiency-oriented design of LLC resonant converters, reducing the need for iterative analytical calculations and time-consuming simulations.
    
    \item The development of a Random Forest-based surrogate model capable of accurately predicting converter efficiency, achieving prediction errors below 1\%.
    
    \item Validation of the proposed approach through detailed simulations, demonstrating strong agreement between predicted and actual converter performance.
\end{itemize}
